Response to Motherhood in crisis by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager and Saidu Bah

This was a difficult piece to write about, not just because of the nature of the subject matter, which is absolutely dreadful, but because relating this back to a common theme seemed an impossible task at first. How does the struggle of women in Sierra Leone to find medical and legal necessities, especially as this was written at the onset of Covid-19, parallel to the larger arm of global crises we are now all facing.

It turns out this is less of a stretch than one might assume. The global south is repeatedly the play-test for how abject poverty will structurally replicate, how power responds and especially power structures that hold less staying capacity against hegemonic superstructures like the Silicon Valley elites and massive military-industrial state machines like the United States, Russia, China, and the European States.

What is illustrated, quite literally, in this graphic novel series, is not unlike what you see unfolding in places like Alabama, or the Siberian region of Russia, or the shadow states of the Chinese infrastructure, like Hong Kong and Yiwu, places that aren’t viewed as particularly valuable to overarching state apparatuses or colonial interest beyond resource extraction or extremely underpaid industrialized labor. Left in pockets of conservative traditionalism, brute-force patriarchy, and mythology enforcement, the will to survive builds out very strange ontological spaces.

Sierra Leone, or parts of South America, India, The Middle East, they are extremely concentrated versions of the same mentality that is illegalizing abortion access and legalizing the conditions to allow for spousal sexual assault in Louisianna at the hands of the preacher-man. The lack of access to resources, because of the shifting of those resources to a privileged few, despite more than enough to go around, doesn’t directly extinguish a population. That void is what creates a terrible symbiotic relationship between the mythological needs of the elites and the material realities of the poor.

The relatability these four stories provide is not just in the medium itself, but also in that it pins a point to logically extrapolate, much like Octavia Butler’s Parable stories did. America can look like Sierra Leone, it already does, you just don’t actively see that or chose to reject it. If you follow the threads of the American Project, or any imperial project built out of a notion that humans are a means to an end or are fodder for disposal, you get to the same end point. Germany did it, Soviet Russia did it, Japan did it, Britain did it, Spain, France, Belgium, Saudia Arabia, South Africa, Israel, Afghanistan, Iran, Pakistan, on and on and on, until, of course, America, all did it or are actively doing it right now.

These stories were about women in poverty and women without access to information and by association, healthcare and legal representation, but the thing that was achieved through this storytelling and visual presentation, whether intentional or not, in its ability to soften the blow while delivering the blow, is that it’s really a warning about what is the inevitable end point in systematically not caring about human beings and not providing them with the simplest access to basic resources and human dignity.

If it can happen anywhere, it can happen anywhere else. Period.
