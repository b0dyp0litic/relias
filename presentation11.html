Control utilizes the alien in the familiar in its complete abstraction of the corporate/ civic office environment. The story is told as much through the shifting and fractaling of the physical space as it is through the narrative itself. This building, this world, is not hospitable to you, the single unifying thing about the bureau of control is your complete lack of control over any aspect of it. Filing cabinets spew files, boilers as big as small houses spit fire, there is an entire massive powerplant sitting at the bureau’s heart, the building feels as big as a city and has no indication what is up or down, east or west, train lines run throughout, rooms open up into chasms. Everything is brutalist but everything is also alive. There is no constraint within our understanding of reality and physics that exist within the walls of this windowless monolith. The only thing we can understand is that it is not for the humans within it.
