Response to Nancy Baker Cahill: Ecological Imagination, Social Sculpture, & Experiential Practice in Polycrisis

I try to avoid speaking from my perspective, but I felt it was necessary for this artist talk as it directly overlaps, not just with themes I have attempted to incorporate in all of my work for this class, but literally my entire raison d'être for the last decade or so. This is specifically why I am back at school at this moment and what the focus of my personal projects are in the space in-between things. She and I appear to be cut from some of the same cloth, and it was incredibly refreshing to hear another person speak so directly and so poignantly about the technological moment, as well as the socio-political moment, that we are being subjected to. She offers realistic frameworks for the use of new technologies for expressive, if not potentially liberatory, means by those of us who are staunchly in opposition to the proposed techno-fascist overseers, who become increasingly more bizarre and brazen by the hour, and their sycophantic lap-puppies who get oogley-eyed over the concentrations of wealth and power that support such an anti-human political project, like some who will remain unnamed, who currently wield governmental power here and abroad.

I was loosely aware of some of Cahill’s work just from being in certain circles but had never really done a deep dive, for whatever reason, until she dove us deep together. I am so impressed with her work now that I know more about the processes and philosophies behind how she creates her pieces. I am always a technology person first. I have grown up nearly fused to computing devices. Everything I do, the way my brain understand the world, come from my relationship to these machines. I had internet before people knew what it was, just a nerd project on DOS in the late 80s/ early 90s, calling out to SETI or getting the weather. I still own a green screen console UNIX, as well as several other vintage PCs. I have ridden the wave from text adventures to virtual reality. I am a believer in the use of these tools, all of them, yes even AI, for the betterment of our circumstances, for the capacity to find new ways to create, and for the potential to collaboratively evolve our intellectual capacity. I always try to see technology as a neutral entity that gains its conceptual framing upon use. Cahill seems to very much share this perspective. The simple fact alone that she prefers to use virtual reality to “paint” or “draw”, to strip the confines of physical limitations, sculpting in 3D space without the need for materials or physics, really made me feel galvanized in a lot of the ideas I have had on these matters.

There tends to be a luddism to a level that starts to feel devolutionary that people have when the associations with technology feel bad and out of control and when a nostalgia for simplicity and structural lubricity take over, in which many people, in my circle and beyond, damn the use of new media technologies and the creative works that are birthed from that use. Cahill doesn’t just use those technologies confidently, she so skillfully creates immersiveness that there isn’t the room to even understand how to build an opposition. Her expansive use of multiple technologies is such a cup and ball game of sacred cows to lay on the altar of techno-primitivism that you are forced to just stop and engage in awe. Her art ruptures all conceptions of how art should be. Her AR work builds things that are larger than buildings, entities that float through open space, massive works of collective public ablution. If she didn’t explain to us the “how” of the pieces, it’d be near impossible to reverse conceptualize what it even is.

Furthermore, everything is as much a criticism of the structures that allow the art to exist in the first place. She is clearly aware, in her purpose for pieces, in the media she chooses, in who she collaborates with, in how the work engages with the public and how the public engages with the work, in how her virtual work intentionally spreads or does not spread on digital platforms, every aspect is unapologetically aware of what the crises of the moment are. The QNTM quote from There Is No Anti-mimetics Division to launch her talk to the quote from Bogna Koniar’s Dark Forest Theory of the Internet, that was dropped almost in passing, she understands the gravity of the conversations that need to be had, right now, and her art is reflective of that and evolves as these crises evolve in real-time. Even the title of her talk speaks to that urgency.

The projects I am working on personally lean heavily into iterations of what could be considered Dark Forests (my main project I house under the title b0dyp0litic which I was also surprised that she has a work under the name body politic as well). The concept, coming from Cixin Liu’s Three Body Problem series, that, especially in the dawn of AI, the internet as we understand it, the digital tapestry that drapes our reality, will remain all pervasive and attempt at some conception of omnipotence, however flawed that might be, and that it is best for us, for our safety, for our sanity, for our shared humanity, and for our capacity to be creative, to take our communities, reduce them in size, go into the trees and turn out the lights. One question I really wish I had asked at the end of her talk, and one that I grapple with constantly, is if there is truth to that theory, how does she, being an artist who works so largely in community engaged, if not collaboratively built, public artwork, reconcile with the idea that it might actually be safest to build out of plain sight? If we need to retreat to the forest, how do we announce to others that necessity and that urgency, without causing unneeded risk?

I respect that she leaves much of the discovery to the viewer. She instills wonder, joy, sadness, anger, pit-in-your-stomach hopelessness alongside overflowing moments of irrational hope. She creates conceptual pathways toward notions of mutual care. She utilizes the unhuman to birth true humanity. Maybe that is the answer right there, you don’t need to tell people where to go and when, you just create the conditions for shared humanness and that pull will lead intuitively. CENTO is still alive after all.
